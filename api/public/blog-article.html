why criminals target small businesses (and exactly how they do it)

sentinelpayjuly 22, 2026 · 4 min read

"we're too small to be worth targeting." i thought that too, then i went through the case files, and i was wrong.

criminals don't chase the big players. big exchanges have entire aml departments, screening tools, compliance officers, and budgets in the millions. going after them is hard work.

going after you is not.

no screening tool. no compliance team. just you, running your business, trying to keep customers happy. to someone moving dirty money, that is an open door.

you're not paranoid, you're the target

the firms that track this for a living keep saying the same thing. trm labs has warned that criminals are deliberately going after smaller operators with tactics that used to be aimed at banks. chainalysis puts it plainly: small businesses don't have the infrastructure to spot laundering, and that is exactly what makes them useful for pushing illicit funds into the real economy.

read that word again. useful. you're not collateral damage in someone else's scheme, you are the scheme.

here is why you fit the profile:

  • you don't screen wallets automatically
  • you don't have time to investigate every payment
  • you trust your clients, because normally that is good business
  • you're not on any regulator's radar
  • you move fast to close deals, so you never stop to ask questions

every one of those is a strength in a normal business. against a laundering operation, every one of them is a hole.

three ways it actually happens

these aren't hypotheticals. they are patterns pulled straight out of chainalysis and elliptic case work.

the new client with the clean story

a new customer shows up wanting your service. 10,000 usdt, paid upfront. they have a website, a linkedin, they even hop on a video call. it all checks out, so you deliver.

three months later tether freezes that 10,000 usdt. it traces back to a sanctioned group. you didn't know. it doesn't matter. your account is frozen and you are the one explaining it to a regulator.

the loyal client with a new wallet every time

you have a customer who pays like clockwork, every month, never late. the only odd thing is a different wallet address each time. "security reasons," they tell you.

what you can't see is that every one of those addresses spins out of the same master key, and that key is tied to a darknet market. you have been settling payments for a criminal operation for half a year without a clue.

the urgent one

a client calls in a panic. needs it now, sends the money, asks you to ship immediately. you are a good operator, so you do.

that payment came out of a ransomware payout. someone paid to get their files back, and the money landed with you. now you are a link in an extortion chain.

policies in a drawer won't save you

elliptic talks about "operationalizing" risk. strip out the jargon and it means doing something on every single transaction, instead of writing a policy and filing it away. complyadvantage makes the same point a different way: nobody gets caught out because they lack an aml policy. they get caught because they can't enforce it on the payment sitting in front of them.

so here is what actually moves the needle.

stop checking wallets by hand. manual checks are slow, inconsistent, and they fall apart the moment you get busy. you miss things. your clients wait. it does not scale past a handful of payments.

check before the money hits your account, not after. that is the whole game. once a bad payment settles, you are doing damage control. before it settles, you still have a choice.

criminals pick you because you don't have the tools to see them coming. that is the only reason, and it is fixable.
